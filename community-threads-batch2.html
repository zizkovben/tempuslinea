DEBATED GLOBE PIN → Yonaguni Submarine Monument #170

Yonaguni: geological accident or submerged architecture?

Opened by pacific_diver_88 · 0 replies · 184 ▲ 47 ▼

I've been diving Yonaguni twice and the thing that gets me every time is the right angles. Nature loves curves — fractures, yes, but sustained orthogonal geometry at that scale feels different. Masaaki Kimura has documented the site for decades and argues in his 1996 paper Man-Made Structures off Yonaguni Island that the terraced platform, the "road," and the arch-like formation are human-modified at minimum, fully constructed at most. His sea-level chronology places submersion between 10,000–8,000 BCE.

Robert Schoch — who physically dived the site — has shifted position over time. He initially leaned natural, citing yonaguni's sandstone fracture patterns, but later acknowledged in Forgotten Civilization (2012) that some features may represent human modification of a natural formation. That's a meaningful concession from someone who built his reputation on rigorous geological scepticism.

Graham Hancock covers Yonaguni extensively in Underworld (2002), situating it within his broader thesis of submerged coastal civilisations lost to post-glacial sea level rise. He notes that mainstream archaeology almost entirely ignores underwater sites despite the fact that 27 million square kilometres of habitable coastal land was inundated between 20,000–7,000 BCE.

What's the community's current read? Natural fracture, human modification, or full construction? And does the answer change depending on your prior assumptions about pre-10,000 BCE civilisational capacity?

THEORIZED EVIDENCE THREAD → YDB Culture #88 · YD Impact Sites #118 · Pre-Flood N. America #172

The Younger Dryas Impact Hypothesis — what's the strongest evidence in 2025?

Opened by cosmicgeology · 0 replies · 312 ▲ 89 ▼

The Younger Dryas Impact Hypothesis (YDIH) proposes that a fragmented comet or asteroid struck the Earth approximately 12,800 years ago, triggering the sudden cold reversal known as the Younger Dryas and causing mass megafaunal extinctions across North America. The hypothesis was formally proposed by Firestone et al. in a landmark 2007 PNAS paper and has been substantially developed by Randall Carlson and others since.

The core physical evidence Carlson documents in his Kosmographia podcast series (particularly episodes 88–120) includes: platinum and iridium spikes in sediment layers at the Younger Dryas Boundary (YDB) across four continents; nano-diamonds and cubic lonsdaleite — a shock-metamorphism mineral only produced by hypervelocity impact — in the same stratigraphic horizon; magnetic spherules consistent with melted extraterrestrial material; and the Carolina Bays, hundreds of thousands of elliptical depressions across the eastern seaboard whose consistent orientation Carlson argues records the ballistic trajectory of a low-angle impact airburst.

Graham Hancock extends this framework in Magicians of the Gods (2015) and America Before (2019), arguing that the impact destroyed or severely disrupted a pre-existing advanced civilisation and that global flood myths — from the Sumerian Epic of Gilgamesh to the Hopi's tales of the previous world — encode cultural memory of this catastrophe.

Critically, a 2018 study published in the Journal of Geology found platinum anomalies at the YDB across South Africa, extending the impact's physical footprint to five continents. This is the kind of falsifiable, peer-reviewed corroboration that strengthens the hypothesis considerably. What evidence do you find most compelling — or most problematic?

DEBATED MYSTERY → Sanxingdui Culture #169

Sanxingdui — who were these people and where did they go?

Opened by bronze_age_watcher · 0 replies · 267 ▲ 31 ▼

The 2021 excavation at Sanxingdui — revealing six new pits packed with gold masks, bronze figures, and objects with no parallel anywhere in the ancient world — might be the most important archaeological discovery of the decade and it's barely in mainstream consciousness. These people were contemporaneous with the Shang Dynasty but utterly alien to it. No shared iconography. No shared writing system. No shared artistic grammar whatsoever.

The bronze masks with their dramatically protruding eyes are the most alien objects in Chinese archaeology. The largest bronze tree — over four metres tall — has no analogue in any culture. The gold sceptre fragments and the bizarre "crawling figure" bronzes look like nothing made anywhere else on Earth at any period.

Then around 1046 BCE — coinciding roughly with the Shang collapse — the Sanxingdui people buried their sacred objects in ritual pits and vanished from the archaeological record. Some scholars suggest they migrated downstream to become the Jinsha culture. Others see deliberate concealment before conquest. The pits feel less like rubbish disposal and more like a sacred cache — as if someone expected to come back.

What theories does the community find credible? Independent parallel development? Contact with a lost maritime civilisation? Something weirder? The 2021 finds include silk residue and ivory — both long-distance trade goods — suggesting Sanxingdui was not isolated.

DEBATED → Göbekli Tepe Builders #168

Göbekli Tepe was deliberately buried — by whom, and why?

Opened by anatolian_deep · 0 replies · 398 ▲ 44 ▼

Göbekli Tepe's deliberate burial around 8000 BCE is one of archaeology's most puzzling facts. Klaus Schmidt, who excavated the site until his death in 2014, confirmed this in Sie bauten die ersten Tempel (2006) — the infill is not gradual accumulation but intentional deposition. Someone chose to bury the world's most sophisticated ritual complex under tonnes of debris. This act of concealment preserved it perfectly for 10,000 years.

The standard explanation is ritual decommissioning — a common practice in which sacred spaces are intentionally closed. But the sheer scale (the buried area likely dwarfs the excavated portion) and the precision of the burial suggest something more deliberate. Andrew Collins argues in Göbekli Tepe: Genesis of the Gods (2014) that the site was built by a Denisovan-influenced population with sophisticated astronomical knowledge, its T-pillars oriented not to the solstice sun but to the star Deneb in Cygnus — a star that marked true north at that epoch due to precession.

Hancock notes in Magicians of the Gods (2015) that the site's construction predates agriculture, overturning the standard model that complex society requires food surplus. If hunter-gatherers built this, the question becomes: what was their civilisational model, and what happened to it? The burial date — approximately 8000 BCE — aligns intriguingly with the tail end of the Younger Dryas cold period.

Was the burial a closing ritual? Concealment from an incoming group? Preservation of sacred knowledge for a future age? The community's theories welcome here.

DEBATED HIGHLY CONTESTED → Bosnian Pyramid Complex #166

The Bosnian Pyramids — definitive thread: evidence for and against

Opened by euro_megaliths · 0 replies · 441 ▲ 612 ▼

This thread is intended as a structured, evidence-focused discussion of the Bosnian Pyramid claim. Personal attacks on Semir Osmanagić or dismissals without engagement will be removed. The goal is to actually evaluate the evidence.

The claim: Semir Osmanagić, in Pyramids of Bosnia (2005) and subsequent excavation reports, argues that Visočica Hill near Visoko is a constructed pyramid with a concrete aggregate surface, internal tunnel networks (the Ravne tunnels), and astronomical orientations. He claims carbon dating of organic material within the tunnels yields dates as old as 25,000 BCE.

Evidence cited by proponents: (1) The hill's triangular profile and near-perfect orientation to cardinal points. (2) Surface stones analysed by materials scientists at the University of Zenica reportedly showing concrete-like composition. (3) The Ravne tunnel network, kilometres long, with what Osmanagić describes as megalithic blocking stones. (4) Claims of unusual electromagnetic and ultrasound readings at the site.

Evidence cited by sceptics: (1) A formal statement by the European Association of Archaeologists (2006) concluding the hills are natural flatiron formations shaped by tectonic folding. (2) Robert Schoch, who visited the site, described the "concrete" blocks as natural conglomerate and the tunnels as likely medieval mining workings. (3) No stratified artefact assemblage consistent with a building civilisation has been recovered from controlled excavation.

Where does the weight of evidence currently sit for you — and has the 2021–2024 excavation season changed anything?

CONFIRMED ACADEMIC → Mississippian Culture #159

Cahokia was bigger than medieval London — so why has almost no one heard of it?

Opened by river_mounds · 0 replies · 529 ▲ 38 ▼

Cahokia, the central city of the Mississippian culture near modern St. Louis, had an estimated population of 15,000–20,000 at its peak around 1100 CE. Contemporary London had roughly 15,000 people. Monk's Mound, the site's central platform mound, contains more earth by volume than the Great Pyramid of Giza. And yet it's almost entirely absent from American popular historical consciousness.

Timothy Pauketat's Cahokia: Ancient America's Great City on the Mississippi (2009) is the most readable scholarly account and documents a remarkably sophisticated urban centre with planned neighbourhoods, a central plaza, a grand avenue, a Woodhenge (circular post alignments tracking solstices), and evidence of large-scale communal feasting events. Pauketat argues Cahokia was not just a city but the site of a religious and political revolution that spread the Mississippian cultural complex across the eastern half of North America.

The collapse around 1300–1400 CE is poorly understood. Leading theories include: prolonged drought linked to the Medieval Warm Period transition; deforestation and agricultural exhaustion of the floodplain; internal social stress from an increasingly stratified society; and epidemic disease — possibly even pre-Columbian contact with Old World pathogens via early Norse or other contact.

This is a confirmed civilisation with extraordinary archaeology that deserves far more attention than it gets. What does the community know about Cahokia's decline?

CONFIRMED → Sogdian Civilization #163

The Sogdians: the most important civilisation you've never heard of

Opened by silk_road_scholar · 0 replies · 348 ▲ 12 ▼

For roughly a millennium — from around 300 BCE to 1000 CE — the Sogdians were the dominant commercial intermediaries of the ancient and medieval world. Based in city-states around modern Samarkand and Bukhara in Uzbekistan, their merchant networks stretched from China to Constantinople. They traded silk, spices, glass, horses, and slaves across the full breadth of the Eurasian continent. And yet they are almost entirely absent from Western historical education.

The most extraordinary window into their world is the Sogdian letters — a cache of private commercial correspondence discovered preserved in a Chinese watchtower in 1907 by Aurel Stein. Dating to around 313 CE, they are the oldest surviving private letters from Central Asia, describing trade conditions, family news, and the chaos of the fall of western China in vivid, human terms. One letter from a merchant's wife describes being abandoned in Dunhuang — stranded, penniless, unable to return home — with a directness that crosses 1,700 years effortlessly.

Their cultural legacy is equally extraordinary: Sogdian script is the direct ancestor of Uyghur script, which became the ancestor of Mongolian and Manchu — so Sogdian is the written ancestor of the scripts used by the Mongol Empire that would later conquer their homeland. The murals of Afrasiab (ancient Samarkand) show a civilisation of extraordinary sophistication and cosmopolitan confidence.

Why are the Sogdians so underrepresented in the historical record compared to their actual importance?

DEBATED DATE CHALLENGE → Tiwanaku Civilization #160

Posnansky's 15,000 BCE date for Tiwanaku — serious astronomy or wishful thinking?

Opened by andean_deep_time · 0 replies · 294 ▲ 387 ▼

Arthur Posnansky's Tiahuanacu: The Cradle of American Man (1945) makes a remarkable claim: using the astronomically-determined obliquity of the ecliptic encoded in the Kalasasaya temple's orientation at Tiwanaku, he calculated that the temple was built when the obliquity matched its current alignment — which he computed as approximately 15,000 BCE, making Tiwanaku contemporaneous with the Upper Palaeolithic.

This is not fringe speculation from an amateur. Posnansky was a respected Bolivian archaeologist who spent decades at the site. His astronomical methodology was reviewed by a commission of the German Astronomical Society in 1926–1930, which found his measurements sound but disputed his conclusion, suggesting the alignment more likely dated to 4000–1500 BCE due to the cyclical nature of the obliquity calculation — the same angle recurs on multiple cycles.

Hancock discusses Posnansky's work in Fingerprints of the Gods (1995), arguing that the German commission's rebuttal doesn't fully resolve the problem because the megalithic construction quality and the presence of extinct Pleistocene fauna in iconography suggest a much earlier date than standard archaeology accepts. Mainstream radiocarbon dating places the main construction phases at 400–900 CE — but critics argue that dates organic material deposited over or near the structures, not the construction event itself.

Is Posnansky's astronomical method fundamentally sound but applied to the wrong cycle? Or is the 15,000 BCE date simply wrong? What would it take to resolve this definitively?

THEORIZED → Antarctic Pre-Ice Civilization #175

The Piri Reis map, Hapgood's crustal displacement, and Antarctica before the ice

Opened by maps_of_ancients · 0 replies · 512 ▲ 298 ▼

The Piri Reis map, compiled by Ottoman admiral Piri Reis in 1513 CE from older source maps, shows a southern landmass that some researchers identify as an ice-free Antarctic coastline — centuries before Antarctica's official discovery in 1820. Charles Hapgood analysed the map in detail in Maps of the Ancient Sea Kings (1966) and concluded that the source data came from an unknown advanced civilisation capable of precise cartography at a global scale. Albert Einstein wrote a foreword to Hapgood's earlier work endorsing the geological plausibility of crustal displacement, though not the specific Antarctic claim.

Hapgood's crustal displacement theory — distinct from plate tectonics — proposes that the entire lithosphere periodically slides over the asthenosphere, relocating the polar zones. In Earth's Shifting Crust (1958), he argued Antarctica was previously positioned at a temperate latitude, supporting civilisation, before a rapid crustal shift iced it over. Rand Flem-Ath developed this thesis in When the Sky Fell (1995), co-written with Graham Hancock, proposing Antarctica as the most likely location of Plato's Atlantis.

The sceptical case is strong: mainstream geology finds no evidence for the rapid large-scale crustal displacement Hapgood requires; the Antarctic features on the Piri Reis map are plausibly reinterpreted as distorted South American coastline; and seismic and ice-core data show no rapid crustal event in the required timeframe. Yet the Antarctic coastline under the ice — revealed by sub-glacial surveys — does show continental features that bear some resemblance to the Piri Reis southern landmass.

Does the Piri Reis map represent ancient knowledge of an ice-free Antarctica, or is that a modern projection onto an ambiguous historical document?

THEORIZED → Adam's Calendar Culture #167 · Anunnaki #120

Adam's Calendar and the southern African gold mines — Anunnaki infrastructure or natural geology?

Opened by southern_megaliths · 0 replies · 387 ▲ 441 ▼

Michael Tellinger's Adam's Calendar (2008), co-authored with Johan Heine, presents a bold case: a stone circle complex in South Africa's Mpumalanga province is the world's oldest astronomical calendar, potentially 75,000–300,000 years old, and part of a vast network of ancient stone ruins that Tellinger interprets as gold-mining infrastructure built by — or for — the Anunnaki described in Zecharia Sitchin's Earth Chronicles (1976–2007).

Tellinger's argument has two separable components. The first — that the Mpumalanga stone ruins are ancient, widespread, and archaeologically underexplored — has more merit than mainstream archaeology has acknowledged. Aerial surveys do show thousands of circular stone enclosures across a vast area, many clearly ancient. The second component — the Anunnaki connection and the extreme age claims — involves Sitchin's interpretation of Sumerian texts as literal accounts of extraterrestrial colonisation, which professional Assyriologists have consistently rejected as a misreading of the source material.

The astronomical orientations of Adam's Calendar itself — including apparent alignment to solstice sunrises and the star Orion — are less controversial. Independent researcher and archaeoastronomer Lenie Holbrook has noted that the site does show intentional orientations. Whether these represent a 75,000-year-old calendar or a much more recent (but still ancient) construction is the key question that proper excavation could address.

Is Tellinger's site being unfairly dismissed because of the Anunnaki framing? And what would good-faith archaeological investigation of the Mpumalanga complex look like?

THEORIZED → Pre-Flood N. American Culture #172 · YDB Culture #88

The hidden pre-flood record of North America — what survived the catastrophe?

Opened by america_before · 0 replies · 463 ▲ 184 ▼

Graham Hancock's America Before (2019) synthesises two decades of research into the proposition that a sophisticated civilisation existed in North America before the Younger Dryas catastrophe of 12,800 BCE and that its traces are systematically underacknowledged by American archaeology — partly due to the legacy of the Clovis First paradigm, which held until the 1990s that humans didn't arrive in the Americas until 13,000 years ago.

Pre-Clovis sites that have now been confirmed include Monte Verde in Chile (14,500 BCE), the Debra L. Friedkin site in Texas (~15,000 BCE), and the Topper Site in South Carolina where controversial lithics may date to much earlier periods. Randall Carlson has focused particularly on the Missoula megaflood evidence — the channelled scablands of Washington State — as physical evidence of the catastrophic landscape transformation that would have destroyed coastal and lowland settlements across the continent.

The mythological evidence is striking: Hancock documents flood narratives among the Ojibwe (the world destroyed by water and fire), the Cherokee (a great flood destroying a previous humanity), and particularly the Hopi, whose oral tradition describes multiple previous worlds destroyed by successive catastrophes. Native American scholars including Vine Deloria Jr. argued in Red Earth, White Lies (1997) that indigenous oral traditions contain genuine deep-time historical memory that orthodox archaeology dismisses too readily.

Serpent Mound in Ohio — now known to have been built or significantly modified around 1070 CE — aligns to the summer solstice sunset, but its location directly over a crypto-explosion structure (a buried meteorite crater) and its comet-like form have led some researchers to connect it to cultural memory of the Younger Dryas impact. What physical or cultural evidence do you find most compelling for pre-YD complexity in North America?
